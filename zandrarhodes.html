Zandra Rhodes

As one of the British "New Wave" designers,
Zandra Rhodes has revolutionized the fashion world and placed London at the forefront of the international fashion scene since the 1970s.
Born in Kent, England, in 1940, Zandra was introduced to the world of fashion through her mother,
who worked as a garment fitter at the House of Worth in Paris and was also a fashion lecturer at Medway College of Art.
After studying textile design at Medway and the Royal College of Art, Zandra initially began her career as a textile designer.
Shortly after, she developed her distinctive fashion designs, teaching herself to create garments on her own terms.
Her background in textile design became a key source of inspiration for her clothing,
allowing the textile patterns to influence the shape of her designs.
Her work was also inspired by various art forms, including paintings by renowned artists
such as Kandinsky, Modigliani, and Matisse,
as well as Elizabethan-era clothing at the Victoria and Albert Museum, Mexican sombreros,
and the safety pins and distressed clothing of Londonâ€™s 1970s punk era.

In recognition of her contributions to the textile and fashion industries,
Zandra was named a Royal Designer for Industry by the Royal Society of Arts in 1994
and was appointed Commander of the British Empire (CBE) by Queen Elizabeth in 1997.
She also designed costumes for productions by the San Diego Opera,
including Wolfgang Mozart's The Magic Flute (2001), the sets and costumes for Georges Bizet's The Pearl Fishers (2004 and 2008),
and was commissioned by the English National Opera and Houston Grand Opera
to design the sets and costumes for Giuseppe Verdi's Aida (2007).

In 1999, Zandra founded the Fashion and Textile Museum in London,
designed by Mexican architect Ricardo Legorreta.
The museum focuses on contemporary British and international fashion, textiles, and jewelry.
Today, Zandra continues to design fashion from her studios in San Diego and London,
producing two collections each year. She also lectures on her experiences in the fashion industry.
Objects from the permanent collection of the Mingei International Museum complement Zandra's dynamic inspiration.
The geometric designs of African Kuba cloth, the vibrant colors of Indian saris,
and the intricate patterns of Indonesian batik highlight the wide spectrum of inspiration for her textile and clothing designs.
